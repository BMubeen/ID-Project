Central Argument

A 150-Year Pattern of Use and Exclusion

Disciplines:

Winnipeg, Manitoba
2025–2026
Canada has consistently recruited immigrant labour to power its economy, then failed to integrate those workers as equals — across every era, across every policy reform.

From the 17,000 Chinese men who died building the CPR for one dollar a day, to the Syrian-trained physicians who spend years working as medical assistants because their credentials aren't recognized — the pattern repeats. The tools of exclusion change. The exclusion does not.

This project traces that history across four chapters: the railway era of explicit exploitation, the prairie settlement era of selective welcome, the 1967 Points System that removed racial barriers on paper, and the modern credential recognition crisis that reveals how much unfinished work remains.

Understanding this history matters because Canada is now one of the most immigration-dependent countries in the developed world. Since 2012, all Canadian labour force growth has come from immigration. Getting immigrant employment right isn't a humanitarian nicety — it's an economic necessity. And we are still getting it wrong.

01
1881–1885 · The Railway Era

You Must Have This Labour,
or You Can't Have the Railway

Prime Minister John A. Macdonald, 1882. The most honest thing the Canadian government ever said about Chinese workers — and the logic that would define immigrant employment for generations.

The Canadian Pacific Railway was the infrastructure that made Canada possible — a ribbon of steel connecting the Pacific coast to the Atlantic, fulfilling the promise that brought British Columbia into Confederation in 1871. Building it required blasting through the Rocky Mountains, a task so dangerous and labour-intensive that contractors couldn't find enough workers willing to do it.

The solution was 17,000 men from the Guangdong and Fujian provinces of southern China. They came for the same reason immigrants have always come to Canada: the promise of work, and the possibility of a better life. Many planned to send money home to families they had left behind. Instead, they built a country that didn't want them to stay.

Chinese workers were paid approximately $1 per day — less than half the wages earned by their white co-workers for the same or more dangerous work. They received no food, no shelter, and no medical care from the CPR. They were assigned the most lethal tasks: drilling through unstable rock, placing explosive charges by hand, hanging from rope baskets over cliff faces. An estimated 600 to 700 died in construction — a figure historians now consider a significant undercount, since Chinese deaths were often not recorded at all.

One of the few surviving first-person accounts comes from Dukesang Wong, who kept a diary during construction. After four years of brutal labour, he wrote: "Times for us now are definitely changing, and the fortunes of our people need great care and guarding." His hope was not rewarded.

Within months of the last spike being driven, the same federal government that called Chinese labour indispensable passed the Chinese Immigration Act of 1885, imposing a $50 head tax. Raised to $100 in 1900 and $500 in 1903 (≈ $18,000 today), the tax was explicitly designed to prevent Chinese workers from forming permanent families in Canada. In 1923, the Chinese Immigration Act effectively banned all Chinese immigration. Canada's formal apology came in 2006 — 121 years after the head tax was imposed.

02
1896–1914 · The Prairie Settlement Era

Only Farmers Need Apply:
The Last Best West

Under Minister Clifford Sifton, Canada launched the most aggressive immigration recruitment campaign in its history — and one of the most explicitly racialized.

By the 1890s, Canada faced a crisis: the prairies were empty. Nearly one million Canadians had emigrated to the United States during the 1880s, attracted by the American Homestead Act. In 1896, PM Wilfrid Laurier appointed Clifford Sifton as Minister of the Interior with a single mandate: fill the prairies.

Sifton's campaign — marketed as "The Last Best West" — was one of the most logistically successful immigration drives in world history. Between 1896 and 1914, more than two million settlers poured into the Canadian prairies. In 1913 alone, 400,870 immigrants arrived — a single-year record that has never been broken. Alberta and Saskatchewan were created in 1905 because enough settlers had arrived to justify provincial status.

Sifton's campaign was explicitly racialized. He believed the ideal immigrant was what he called "a stalwart peasant in a sheepskin coat" — specifically Ukrainians, Germans, and Scandinavians with farming experience. African Americans with extensive farming experience were actively turned away by government agents. Asian immigrants remained subject to the head tax. The categories of labour were rigidly enforced: farmers first, domestic servants second.

The government's 1899 archive at Library and Archives Canada documents the systematic import of single British women aged 17 to 35 as household servants. By 1906, 4,467 women per year entered Canada this way. They were recruited by government-affiliated organizations like the Girls' Friendly Society and placed with upper-class families in Toronto, Winnipeg, and Vancouver — low-paid, legally vulnerable, and largely forgotten in the historical record.

Then came WWI. Overnight, 80,000 Ukrainian Canadians who had settled the prairies were classified as "enemy aliens." Over 5,000 were interned in forced-labour camps. The same people Canada had specifically recruited to build its breadbasket were locked up. Canada did not formally recognize this injustice until 2008.

03
1967 · The Points System

Merit Over Race:
The Promise and the Problem

The 1967 Immigration Regulations replaced race-based selection with a points system — Canada's most significant immigration reform, and the beginning of new, more subtle inequities.

By the early 1960s, Canada's racial immigration architecture was increasingly difficult to justify publicly. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948), the civil rights movement, and Canada's own Bill of Rights (1960) had changed the landscape. In 1967, the Pearson government passed the Points System.

Independent applicants would be assessed on nine criteria — education, personal character, occupational demand, occupational skill, age, pre-arranged employment, knowledge of French and English, relatives in Canada, and destination employment opportunities — and those scoring 50 or more out of 100 would be admitted. No mention of race or nationality. The reform was genuine: before 1967, over 90% of immigrants came from Europe. Between 2006 and 2011, Asia and the Middle East accounted for 56.9% of new arrivals.

But the system created a new problem. Canada was now selecting engineers, doctors, lawyers, and accountants from around the world based on their qualifications. It had no mechanism to ensure those qualifications would be recognized once immigrants arrived. The Points System was built for admission — not for integration.

Immigrants were admitted because of their credentials. They arrived to find those credentials didn't transfer. They were told their education was "equivalent" but their "Canadian experience" was insufficient. They were overqualified for low-skill jobs and under-recognized for professional positions. Canada redesigned who it would let in — without redesigning how it would help them thrive.

04
2000s–2025 · The Modern Era

Brain Waste:
The Crisis That Keeps Being Rediscovered

Today, Canada admits more immigrants than ever — and continues to systematically underemploy them. The tools of exclusion have changed. The pattern has not.

In 2025, Canada faces a paradox that would be darkly familiar to a Chinese railway worker in 1885: the country needs you, brings you here for your labour, and then creates barriers that prevent you from contributing what you actually know how to do.

The modern version of this story is called "brain waste" — the systematic underemployment of highly skilled immigrants in jobs far below their qualifications. According to a 2025 C.D. Howe Institute report, overqualified immigrants earn on average 46% less than non-immigrants working in the same fields they trained for. The overqualification rate among recent immigrants is three times higher than among Canadian-born workers. Over 25% of immigrants with foreign degrees work in jobs that require only a high school diploma.

The barriers are structural. In regulated professions — medicine, engineering, nursing, law — foreign credentials must be assessed by provincial licensing bodies. These processes are complex, expensive, often opaque, and can take years. A physician trained in India may spend three years working as a medical assistant while navigating a requalification maze that costs thousands of dollars with no guaranteed outcome. Many give up. Many leave. "Reverse brain drain" — skilled immigrants departing for the US or Australia — has reached its highest levels in two decades.

The continuity is remarkable. In 1885, Chinese workers were excluded by law the moment the railway was done. In 1904, British women were imported as domestic servants. In 1967, skilled workers from Asia were admitted for the first time — then found their skills weren't recognized. In 2025, Canada selects the world's most credentialed immigrants through Express Entry, then watches them work cashier shifts while their engineering degrees sit on a shelf. The tools have changed. The pattern has not.
